About

A Ph. D. in psychology I have extensive experience in individual, group therapy, and psychoeducation. I have worked with children, youth, adults, and families for individual and group therapy providing services for various types of distress, depression, anxiety, family conflicts, career struggles, workplace stress, and similar psychosocial issues. I understand the multicultural dynamics and work with diverse, and immigrant populations.

A recipient of the Fulbright Award, I have international exposure to the best practices in the field. A member of various professional associations, such as the American Psychological Association, The Ontario Association of Mental Health Professionals, and the World Health Organization’s (WHO) Global Clinical Practice, I am keen to update my skills and learn about the latest in the field of psychotherapy.

My experience include numerous psycho-education programs conducted for academic institutions (Faculty and students), Corporate groups, and parenting skills training. Spreading information about mental health and enabling the community to look beyond stigma and seek help whenever there is any mental distress; is my professional objective.

Committed to providing a compassionate and non-judgmental space for collaborative work, I welcome you to the comfort of a safe and trusting place, to share and understand through our therapeutic alliance. By working on mutually agreed upon goals, I focus on empowering the clients through increased awareness of their inner patterns and find the courage to face problems, and regain a sense of purpose.

Some of the signs of an anxiety disorder are-

  • • Fear or anxiety is out of proportion to the actual threat.
  • • Difficulty to control the worry.
  • • Feeling restlessness, keyed up, or on edge.
  • • Being easily fatigued.
  • • Difficulty concentrating or mind going blank.
  • • Irritability.
  • • Sleep disturbance (difficulty falling or staying asleep, or restless, unsatisfying sleep).

    • Some of the signs of Depression are-
  • • Sad, empty, or irritable mood.
  • • Out of control behavior, or recurrent temper outbursts.
  • • Diminished interest or pleasure in all, or almost all, activities.
  • • Inability to sleep, or oversleeping.
  • • Psychomotor agitation such as feelings of restlessness, or being slowed down.
  • • Fatigue or loss of energy nearly every day.
  • • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive or inappropriate.
  • • Thoughts of death, recurrent suicidal ideation.
  • • Inability to function in our social or work situation.

FAQs

What is the process of Psychotherapy?

Psychotherapy is a collaborative treatment based on the relationship between an individual and a psychotherapist. Almost all types of psychotherapy involve developing a therapeutic relationship, communicating and creating a dialogue, and working to overcome problematic thoughts, feelings or behaviors.

How does Psychotherapy effect one’s feelings?

Psychotherapy aims to increase an individual's sense of well being and relationships. Psychotherapy is based on trust and the development of a working alliance. The client is empowered to reflect on inner self through exploring one's feelings, thoughts, life situations and behavior patterns.

Who can provide Psychotherapy?

The individuals who are licensed to practice psychotherapy are called Registered Psychotherapists. In addition, psychotherapy in Ontario is also provided by professionals in related disciplines such as psychiatry, psychology, or social work.

How do I know that I need help?

Some of the signs that hint at the need for psychotherapy include feeling overwhelmed, hopeless, irritable, or anxious. Also, having emotional struggles that are disturbing your routine work such as eating, sleeping, exercise and/or your ability to function at work, at school, at home, or in relationships. However, if you have more doubts, book a 15 minutes free consultation.




What if I don’t feel comfortable about discussing a personal issue or disclose an experience?

Psychotherapists are sensitive to the client's needs and employ a wide range of skills to facilitate the client's goals. Psychotherapists are trained to be objective, neutral and nonjudgmental. It is entirely upto the client to seek help on one or the other concern, share information or personal feelings or disagree to the modality used by the psychotherapist. A clear open communication is what any psychotherapist would encourage because it is essential for a healthy therapeutic alliance.
